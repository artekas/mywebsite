Turtle Games: Customer Loyalty Analysis

LSE Project

Python R View Code on GitHub

Project Brief

I conducted an in-depth analysis of Turtle Games’ customer base to uncover what drives long-term loyalty in a highly competitive gaming market. Using Python, R, and NLP techniques, I explored behavioural trends, spending patterns, and customer sentiment to reveal who the most loyal players are and what makes them stay. This project wasn’t just about numbers. It aimed to deliver meaningful insights that can help Turtle Games improve retention, personalise incentives, and shape smarter growth strategies.

Objectives

The analysis was designed to support three strategic questions:

  • What traits predict loyalty?
    Demographic and behavioural data was explored to pinpoint which players are most committed to the brand.
  • Can we forecast future loyalty?
    A predictive model was developed to estimate loyalty points based on core user traits like age, spending score, and income.
  • How can this inform loyalty strategy?
    Insights were used to suggest targeted incentives for different segments.

Exploratory Analysis

To understand what drives loyalty at Turtle Games, four key areas were explored:

  • Demographics like age and income
  • Spending behaviour, especially their spending score, which reflects how actively they purchase
  • Loyalty points, which we used as the measure of engagement
  • And customer sentiment based on reviews

1. EDA revealed a right-skewed distribution in loyalty points, indicating that while many customers earned a moderate number of points, a smaller subset earned exceptionally high amounts. This pattern is typical of loyalty programs, where a relatively small proportion of customers often drive the bulk of revenue and engagement.

Chart: Distribution of Loyalty Points, a classic 80/20 split.

2. A scatterplot of spending score versus loyalty points showed a strong positive correlation. This confirmed that customers with higher engagement and purchasing power tended to earn more points, reinforcing the assumption that spending is a key loyalty driver.

Chart: Spending score and loyalty points were tightly linked, a strong early signal.

3. Income showed a moderate positive relationship with loyalty. While high-income customers often earned more points, the pattern was more scattered compared to spending score. This suggests that income alone doesn’t guarantee loyalty, it's likely a supporting factor that enables, but doesn’t drive, strong engagement.

Chart: Higher income is associated with greater loyalty, but not always. The relationship is real, but less consistent than spending.

4. Customers in the 31–40 age group had the highest average loyalty, but younger players (21–30) weren’t far behind, a key growth segment. They’re showing solid engagement, and they are still early in their customer lifecycle. That means this group is full of untapped potential. They could become Turtle Games’ most valuable players in the years to come.

Chart: The emerging power of young adults.

5. Customers were segmented based on spending behaviour and income using K-means clustering. Five clear groups emerged, each with distinct loyalty profiles. While most segments clustered around average spending, one stood out most, Bargain Hunters earned significantly more loyalty points despite lower income and spending scores. The charts below visualise this segmentation and its loyalty impact.

Predictive Modelling

The model delivered an R² of 0.84, explaining 84% of the variation in loyalty. That’s solid. But it’s not just the number that matters, it’s the meaning:

  • Spending score was the number one driver. Every point increase = ~34.5 more loyalty points.
  • Income helped too, contributing around 33.8 points per step.
  • Age mattered a bit (~12 points), but behaviour clearly outweighed demographics.

While R² gave a sense of overall accuracy, it doesn’t show how the model performs across individuals. Below charts do.

  • The residuals chart shows prediction errors are centered around zero and form a roughly normal shape. This means the model isn’t consistently underestimating or overestimating which is a good sign of prediction balance.

Sentiment Analysis

Next, I conducted sentiment analysis to explore how players express emotion in their reviews and how this might relate to loyalty.

  • Polarity scores from VADER revealed that 90% of reviews were positive. This suggests that Turtle Games enjoys strong brand advocacy: a key advantage in a competitive market.
  • Subjectivity scores via TextBlob indicated that 83% of reviews were moderately subjective, meaning players balanced emotional feedback with useful factual content.
  • Interestingly, negative sentiment was rare (just 6%) and often came with rich detail, signaling not just dissatisfaction, but engagement and investment in the brand.

These insights highlight a loyal, expressive player base. Positive sentiment dominates, but even critical reviews reflect players who care and that’s an opportunity, not a threat.

Data-Driven Recommendations

Based on behavioural patterns, sentiment signals, and predictive modelling, the following recommendations are designed to strengthen loyalty, improve targeting, and guide product innovation at Turtle Games.

1. Loyalty Strategy & Incentives

  • Launch dynamic loyalty tiers based on behavioural segmentation. Reward Bargain Hunters (low spenders, high loyalty) with exclusive tier bonuses to lock in their surprising value.
  • Introduce milestone-based streak rewards to gamify retention among mid-loyalty players (especially the 21–30 age segment). Their rising loyalty indicates long-term growth potential.
  • Create loss aversion triggers, e.g. “You’re 50 points away from Gold” — leveraging positive momentum from existing high scorers.

2. Sentiment-Led Engagement

  • Monitor detailed negative reviews: Only 6% of feedback was negative, but these were detailed and emotional, meaning they came from highly engaged users. Reach out with surveys or invite-only beta access.
  • Celebrate expressive users by incorporating community-sourced testimonials into campaigns, they’re both loyal and vocal.
  • Build feedback loops that reward review submissions, especially from moderately subjective reviewers who blend emotion and facts (83% of reviews fall here).

3. Segment-Based Targeting

  • Double down on “Bargain Hunters”. This cluster drives loyalty despite lower spending and income. They're undervalued and highly responsive. Prioritise them with tailored upgrade paths and group-based referral bonuses.
  • Test differentiated incentives across your five customer clusters. Use predicted loyalty scores to allocate marketing spend more efficiently.
  • Design youth-centric offers for 21–30 year-olds, the most promising emerging segment. They already show high loyalty but aren’t yet being rewarded for it.

3. Technical Improvements & Data Opportunities

  • Enhance clustering accuracy by including product-level interaction data (not just spending/income).
  • Track changes in predicted loyalty over time to proactively identify at-risk users or new high-potential players.
  • Incorporate review timestamp and in-game behaviour to match spikes in loyalty with real events or product features.

Conclusion

This project uncovered more than just drivers of loyalty. It revealed the power of behavioural insight in building long-term engagement. The analysis showed that spending score, not demographics, is the strongest predictor of loyalty. It highlighted clusters like the “Bargain Hunters,” who outperform expectations and represent untapped value. It also demonstrated that players aren’t just loyal, they’re emotionally invested, as seen through overwhelmingly positive sentiment.

By combining predictive modelling, clustering, and sentiment analysis, Turtle Games now has a roadmap to act, not just observe. Loyalty isn’t random, in fact it’s predictable. And with the right incentives, targeting, and feedback loops, it can be amplified. Turtle Games has the data. Now it has the strategy.
